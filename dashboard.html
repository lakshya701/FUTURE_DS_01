05

Insights & Recommendations

What I'd tell the business if I were advising them directly.

1. Furniture is a revenue trap, not a profit driver

Furniture generated $5.18M in revenue — almost as much as Technology's $5.98M — but returned only 2.3% margin versus Technology's 14.8%. Tables (–$99K) and Bookcases (–$34K) are outright loss-making at the sub-category level. Recommendation: audit supplier pricing and discounting on Tables/Bookcases before promoting them further, or reposition them as loss-leaders that pull in Technology/Office Supplies add-on sales.

2. Technology is the most efficient category — lean into it

Technology converts revenue to profit almost 6x more efficiently than Furniture. Office Machines and Telephones & Communication are the two strongest sub-categories by both revenue and profit. Recommendation: shift marketing spend and floor/homepage placement toward Technology, and use it to anchor bundle offers.

3. Revenue has declined since its 2009 peak

Annual revenue fell from $4.21M (2009) to $3.44M (2011) before a modest recovery to $3.72M in 2012 — a net ~12% decline over four years, even though profit stayed relatively stable near $350–435K a year. Recommendation: investigate whether this is customer churn, pricing pressure, or category mix shift before assuming "more marketing" is the fix.

4. Ontario and the Prairies punch above their weight

Ontario delivers the best profit ($347K) despite West having higher revenue ($3.60M vs $3.06M) — Ontario and Prairie both run ~11.3% margins versus the West's 8.3%. Nunavut and Northwest Territories are too small to matter yet (combined <7% of revenue). Recommendation: study what Ontario/Prairie do differently (product mix, discount discipline) and apply it to West, the highest-revenue but comparatively low-margin region.

5. Corporate is the most valuable segment

Corporate customers drive $5.50M in revenue and $600K in profit — more than double any other segment's profit contribution. Recommendation: build a retention/loyalty motion specifically for Corporate accounts, since losing even a few of these customers would materially hurt the bottom line.

6. December is peak season — staff and stock for it

December ($1.47M) and January ($1.44M) are the two strongest months, roughly 30% above the June low ($1.03M). Recommendation: front-load inventory and support staffing ahead of Q4, and consider a targeted January promotion to smooth the post-holiday dip.
